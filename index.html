Position Guide What each role actually means
GK
Goalkeeper
The last line of defence. Modern goalkeepers are expected to be comfortable with the ball at their feet and act as a sweeper behind a high defensive line. Shot-stopping remains primary but distribution — short and long — separates the elite.
CB
Centre Back
The defensive anchor. Can be split into the ball-playing CB — comfortable stepping out with the ball — and the more traditional defensive CB focused on winning duels and organizing the line. The best do both.
FB / WB
Full Back / Wing Back
The modern full back is one of the most demanding positions in football. Expected to defend one-on-one, provide width in attack, combine in tight spaces, and cover enormous distances. Wing backs in a back five push even higher and function almost as wingers.
DM / 6
Defensive Midfielder
The shield in front of the defence. Tasked with breaking up play, winning second balls, and transitioning quickly. The best no. 6s also have the range of passing to start attacks from deep — they are the connective tissue of a team.
CM / 8
Central Midfielder
The engine. Box-to-box midfielders cover every blade of grass. The no. 8 role varies enormously by system — some are essentially second strikers, others are primarily defensive. What unites them: athleticism, decision-making, and the ability to affect both halves.
Regista
Deep-Lying Playmaker
The conductor. Drops deep to receive, rarely presses, and controls the tempo of a match through the weight and timing of their passing. Pirlo is the archetype. Requires elite technical quality and exceptional positional intelligence to compensate for defensive limitations.
AM / 10
Attacking Midfielder / No. 10
The creative hub. Operates in the pockets between midfield and attack. The classic no. 10 is a dying breed in pressing-heavy systems, but the role has evolved — modern 10s are often expected to press aggressively and track back as well as create and score.
W / 7 / 11
Winger
Wide attackers who can play as traditional wide men hugging the touchline and delivering crosses, or inverted wingers who cut inside onto their stronger foot to shoot or combine. The latter has become dominant. Pace, dribbling, and end product are the defining qualities.
CF / 9
Centre Forward / Striker
The finisher. The penalty-box striker is purely about goals — positioning, movement, and composure. The complete centre forward also brings others into play, holds up the ball, and presses from the front. The false nine drops deep to create overloads and space.
False 9
False Nine
A striker who drops into midfield, dragging centre backs out of position and creating space for runners from deep. Messi's greatest Barcelona years were built around this role under Guardiola. Requires a player comfortable both creating and finishing.
IW
Inverted Winger
A winger who starts wide but cuts inside rather than crossing. A left-footed player on the right wing, or vice versa. The inversion creates shooting lanes and overloads in central areas. Robben was the prototype. Now one of the most common roles in the modern game.
Mezzala
Mezzala
A central midfielder who operates in the half spaces — the channels between the full back and centre back. Runs beyond the striker, arrives late into the box, and combines in tight spaces wide. Requires both the technical quality of a 10 and the athleticism of an 8.
